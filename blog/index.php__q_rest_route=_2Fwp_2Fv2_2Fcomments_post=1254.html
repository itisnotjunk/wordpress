The crisis in the peer-reviewed literature is not only about prestige, tradition and academic conservatism, but also the control of information. Academic administrators can get carried away with the power they have in setting access to such a precious resource. This is articulated in The Conversation in this article (which seems to seriously refer to the public as the hoi polloi!)
\nhttp:\/\/theconversation.edu.au\/open-access-and-scholarly-publishing-is-it-time-to-tear-down-the-paywalls-3986<\/a>
\nas
\n\u2018If we have a valuable product, shouldn\u2019t we protect access to it?\u2019
\nIn democracies, Freedom of Information laws are supposed to protect us from this problem. As I long-term observer of the literature crisis I have wondered about the relevance of FOI law and finally, frustrated at still being unable to access the literature, decided to properly investigate, at least in Australia.
\nIn this country most jurisdictions have recently updated their FOI laws to improve the public\u2019s right of access to documents held by public agencies (among which universities are definitely included). Some of the new laws are called Right to Information and require proactive release unless there is a good reason against it. Even under older laws, though, the practice of universities withholding their research publications from the public, especially upon request, is not on solid legal ground and a test case in this area could well appear.
