Lander may well have shot himself in the foot with this article. Let me elaborate:<\/p>\n

Although the intention of Lander in writing this article is clear, it has shed light into a much more important issue that nobody was paying attention to: all the knowledge of CRISPR was already on the table when Doudna-Zhang jumped in to just make the final step and convert it into a gene editing tool. It’s sad but here in Spain nobody knew who Francisco Mojica was until this article came out in Cell. Now, everyone here is talking about our next Nobel Prize. I thought it was just chauvinism until I started reading about the discovery story (mainly Lander’s article\u2026). I have used the CRISPR tools for gene editing myself, extensively, and I did not know anything about these guys (Mojica, Horvath, Siksnys, van der Oost\u2026). Instead, I thought that Doudna-Zhang developed CRISPR almost from scratch, with the help of only some small previous insights from the field of microbiology. But it was indeed the work of the microbiologists who struggled with the lack of funding, the rejection of the groundbreaking idea, the publication bias, the ones who believed all the time the idea of an adaptive immune system in bacteria, along with the subsequent work of those who deciphered every step of the mechanism. Those who made it possible are the ones who deserve the prizes, the glory, the credit… And we know that now because of Lander\u2019s \u201cThe Heroes of CRISPR\u201d article (who knew it before this article besides maybe those in the field of microbiology?), even if it\u2019s clearly biased. At least this is my conclusion after reading the paper: as useful as Doudna-Zhang lab work has been, I don’t see it a real part of the intellectual contribution that has changed (and will continue changing) the way we do science. Don\u2019t get me wrong, this last step has been extremely useful, but it is more the kind of work that Life Sciences could do developing a new kit to help us carry out our lab protocols in our everyday bench work: clone the pieces in plasmids, overexpress in cells, and voil\u00e0.<\/p>\n

One last thing, there is a sentence in Lander’s article that explains why Lander himself, Doudna, Zhang, etc. and the rest of “big labs” were not there at any moment during the CRISPR discovery process. Quoting from Lander\u2019s article: “Like Mojica, Philippe Horvath could hardly have chosen a thesis topic that was more local or less sexy…”. Lander is unconsciously giving us the key of why CRISPR came out of small labs all around the world and not from the big \u201cscience leaders\u201d: these big labs don’t understand that the greatest of the discoveries can be hidden inside an apparently “ugly” hypothesis. I know very well what I\u2019m saying: I worked at one of this \u201cbig labs\u201d for several years and I was in many meetings in which my supervisors rejected my ideas promptly by saying “that’s not sexy” or even “that’s not a Nature” (or even by just saying \u201cbullshit\u201d, believe it or not). These labs work like this: they choose to explore very attractive ideas, with almost no grounds but the kind of ideas you can easily picture in Nature\u2019s cover (we could call them \u201crisky\u201d, I prefer to call them crazy ideas). And then they work them out all the way until they get to Nature or Cell, no matter what the results of the experiments showed (please take a look to Pubpeer to see what I am talking about\u2026). Alternatively, they can take something that\u2019s almost ready, and put a huge amount of money and researchers\/time to finish it before anyone else in the world can, taking advantage of their excellent connections with top journals and their huge funding. The latter was the case of Doudna-Zhang role in CRISPR, in my opinion.<\/p>\n

But even if nowadays that\u2019s sadly what succeeds in terms of publication-funding-positions, that’s not science. Science is what’s waiting for you in a hidden corner on your way to hypothesis rejection (9 out of 10 hypotheses should, statistically, be rejected). Like crispr was waiting for Mojica in a hidden corner of his initially unappealing and probably unproductive \u201cugly\u201d hypothesis about the genome of archeas growing in high salt environments. A hypothesis that Lander, Doudna, Zhang, et al would probably have never explored. So, even if Lander was trying to make the case for Zhang\u2019s Nobel Prize, in my opinion he has helped doing the opposite: he has shown us how a legion of researchers in small labs believed and worked hard in an initially unappealing idea that turned out to be one of the biggest discoveries of science history, doing a great job of collaborative science of outstanding quality. And how the guys that are always there ready to jump in and take the credit did it too late this time\u2026 I really hope that the Nobel Prize committee will also see this.<\/p>\n"},"link":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/?p=1825&cpage=1#comment-1502848","status":"approved","type":"comment","author_avatar_urls":{"24":"https:\/\/secure.gravatar.com\/avatar\/a0615b1ac832f2edf2636b74dd6b19c9?s=24&d=mm&r=g","48":"https:\/\/secure.gravatar.com\/avatar\/a0615b1ac832f2edf2636b74dd6b19c9?s=48&d=mm&r=g","96":"https:\/\/secure.gravatar.com\/avatar\/a0615b1ac832f2edf2636b74dd6b19c9?s=96&d=mm&r=g"},"meta":[],"_links":{"self":[{"href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=\/wp\/v2\/comments\/1502848"}],"collection":[{"href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=\/wp\/v2\/comments"}],"up":[{"embeddable":true,"post_type":"post","href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=\/wp\/v2\/posts\/1825"}],"children":[{"href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=%2Fwp%2Fv2%2Fcomments&parent=1502848"}]}}]
