Jayman points to a fact that is important why Wade is of so much interest: “There is no biological basis for race” is a mantra put up early by the human genome project. It is by most non-scientist and also social scientists equated with: “Geneticists have shown that there are no biological differences between races.” Time and again I am contradicted by well-read lay people when I state that a genetic profile can tell not only race, but even if a person is Ashkenazi Jewish, or German rather than French European – the reference to the FBI mentioned above. This previous denial of the link between biology and race underlies much of the misunderstandings. <\/p>\n

I am aware that the exact boundaries are not identical – on a gross level, e.g. Indians both genetically and linguistically are closer to Europeans than to Asians. <\/p>\n

Clearly, as the examples of Bill Mitchell point out, for many factors the environment (which includes chance or historical opportunities) plays a larger role than genes. <\/p>\n

Bill Mitchell, thanks of reminding us – I would like to add to your list – not withstanding the truth of every one of your statements:<\/p>\n

1) Scientists have found many biological differences between races, and even between small ethnic groups within the races. Some genetic differences are absolute such as genetic variants that only exist in one group, which are used as “ancestry informative markers”, but most overlap between races but differ by frequency, and only in aggregate allow to the pinpointing of one genetic group. Some of these genetic differences undoubtedly will have some effect on phenotype differences.<\/p>\n

2) Parsimony: If there are genetic differences between races not only in skin color, but also in ability to digest alcohol or milk, height, running ability, sweating, metabolizing drugs, blood diseases, isn’t it obvious that some genetic differences between races will also affect behavior? Why should IQ, brain size, impulsivity etc. be exempt from these genetic differences? To call anyone who states this a racist is not helping!<\/p>\n

3) How much of a variation in a trait is influenced by genes, the trait’s heritability, is also effected by the environment: When you take upper middle class twins who are willing to spend a week in a lab to be evaluated, you find most of IQ is affected by genes, probably because these twins are all in a similar, positive environment, so the differences that REMAIN are likely genetic. In contrast, studies have shown that heritability of IQ is very low in a lower SES environment – in other words, in a bad environment, no matter how good or bad the genes, bad environment is bad for your brain, and how bad the environment then becomes the most important to explain differences (some of this taken from Turkheimer 2003). <\/p>\n

4) As pointed out earlier, Flynn effect and many other data make it clear that for most of human history, the environment was vastly different from now, and studies investigating genes and the environmental interaction usually find the effect of environment much stronger than the effect of genes, or of the G x E interaction. It is much harder for evolution to act on G selection when E is constantly changing. <\/p>\n
