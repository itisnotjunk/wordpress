Like everybody, I have a series of interests, financial and otherwise, that could be affected by things I do and opine on. I endeavor not to let these influence my actions as a scientist or commentator. But I am not naive enough to think that I can always do this, and feel it is important that people know the things that might be influencing me.<\/p>\n

Financial<\/strong><\/span>:<\/p>\n

Impossible Foods<\/strong><\/span>: I am an advisor to Impossible Foods, a company founded by my former postdoctoral advisor Patrick Brown, to develop plant-based alternatives to foods derived from animal projects.\u00a0I got involved in the company because\u00a0animal farming has a massively negative effect on the planet, and because I believe we can reduce this negative impact by offering consumers products that satisfy their desire for meat, cheese and other dairy products that have less of an impact on the environment. I spend time on the company because I believe deeply in its mission, but I also receive a small stipend for my work and own equity.<\/p>\n

Areas of biggest conflict: impact of animal farming; food policy; food biotechnology.<\/p>\n

23andMe<\/strong><\/span>: I used to serve on the Scientific Advisory Board of 23andMe, a company that provides\u00a0consumers with information on their DNA through genotyping and through a website that offers information on ancestry and ties the unique collection of DNA variants they contain to the emerging scientific literature on the effect of these mutation. I do not hold any equity in the company, but I support their mission and my long affiliation with them may constitute a conflict of interest.<\/p>\n

Areas of biggest conflict: government regulation of personal genetics; value of personal genetic information to consumers.<\/p>\n

Non-financial<\/strong><\/span>:<\/p>\n

Public Library of Science<\/strong><\/span>: For 20 years I have been a public advocate for reforming the way scientist communicate, and much of my work in this domain focused on the Public Library of Science, a non-profit publisher of open-access scientific and medical journals on whose board of directors I served from 2002 – 2018. My work for PLOS was strictly on a volunteer basis: At now point did I receive any compensation from\u00a0the company for my\u00a0role. I did receive t-shirts and pens from PLOS that are also available to members of the public (but, honestly, I don’t like this corporate stuff and the large collection of PLOS-themed t-shirts people actually see me wearing were made and paid for personally). However,\u00a0despite having no financial interest in PLOS, I have put a huge amount of blood, sweat and tears into the company. I believe fervently in its mission and have an obvious personal stake in its success, even though I am no longer affiliated with them.<\/p>\n

Areas of biggest conflict: Open access policy; anything to do with scientific publishing.<\/p>\n

Professional<\/strong><\/span>:<\/p>\n

Science funding<\/strong><\/span>: As a working scientist who has received grants from a variety of government funding agencies, I have a professional interest in promoting science funding and in influencing the way research funds are distributed. Anyone who follows me knows that I speak my mind freely on the NIH and other funding agencies and their problems, but I will admit that I\u00a0used to pull my punches occasionally for fear that it would influence my prospects of funding.<\/p>\n

I currently receive virtually all of my lab’s funding from the Howard Hughes Medical Institute, and thus clearly have a professional conflict when talking about HHMI.<\/p>\n

Institutional<\/strong><\/span>: I work at the University of California, Berkeley, and have an institutional conflict of interest on anything dealing with UCB, with the University of California write large, educational funding in California, and, arguably, public higher education policy at the national and state level.<\/p>\n

I also have a conflict of interest when talking about areas where UC, UCB or my department (Molecular and Cell Biology) have a financial interest, especially on patents. Understanding CHORUS<\/span><\/b><\/b><\/p>\n

What is CHORUS?<\/b><\/div>\n
\n
    \n
  • The Clearinghouse for the Open Research of the United States (CHORUS) is a framework for a possible public-private partnership to increase public access to peer-reviewed publications that report on federally-funded research.\u00a0 Conceived by publishers, CHORUS would:<\/li>\n
  • Provide a full solution for agencies to comply with the OSTP memo on public access to peer-reviewed scientific publications reporting on federally-funded research<\/li>\n
  • Build on publishers’ existing infrastructure to enhance public access to research literature, avoiding duplication of effort, minimizing cost to the government and ensuring the continued availability of the research literature<\/li>\n
  • Serve the public by creating a streamlined, cohesive way to expand access to articles reporting on federally-funded research.\u00a0 These will be digital forms of peer-reviewed manuscripts or documents and be archived and preserved<\/li>\n
  • Support funding agencies in fulfilling the OSTP directive to provide public access, use public-private partnerships where possible and avoid extra-bud<\/a>getary costs; CHORUS would require little to no federal funding<\/li>\n
  • Utilize current and developing tools, resources and protocols for discoverability, search, archiving and preservation (such as CrossRef, FundRef and ORCID), ensuring continued innovation in the delivery of scholarly communication<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div>\n
    What would CHORUS do?<\/b><\/div>\n
    \n
      \n
    • Identify journal articles resulting from federal funding<\/li>\n
    • Enable a reader to access, free of charge, a full-text version of all peer-reviewed articles reporting on the results of federally funded research<\/li>\n
    • Streamline compliance for authors and funding agencies by integrating public access into the publishing system<\/li>\n
    • Provide an open structure to enable discovery and search through familiar, commercial search engines such as Google Scholar and Microsoft Academic Search as well as government and library search systems<\/li>\n
    • Ensure ongoing and longterm access to and preservation of these articles and their updates<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div>\n
      Who is involved in CHORUS?<\/b><\/div>\n
      \n
        \n
      • CHORUS represents a broad-based group of scholarly publishers, both commercial and not-for-profit.\u00a0 This group collectively publishes the vast majority of the articles reporting on federally-funded research<\/li>\n
      • CHORUS includes a resource partner CrossRef (details on CrossRef and FundRef follow), a nonprofit organization responsible for enabling linking among the world’s scholarly publishing platforms<\/li>\n
      • Publishers involved in CHORUS have engaged with funding agencies subject to the OSTP memo and are in continuing discussions to determine how CHORUS can suit agency needs<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div>\n
        Why CHORUS?<\/span><\/b><\/div>\n
        <\/div>\n
        What is the benefit of CHORUS for publishers?<\/b><\/div>\n
        \n
          \n
        • The mission of scholarly publishers is to disseminate knowledge.\u00a0 The advancement of public access aligns with that mission<\/li>\n
        • CHORUS leverages publisher investments in a proven infrastructure for identifying, accessing and archiving journal articles<\/li>\n
        • CHORUS minimizes additional compliance costs for publishers<\/li>\n
        • CHORUS enables publishers to offer free public access in context while maintaining publishers’ continued access to information about article usage so that publishers\u00a0 can continue their critical role in science communication and innovation<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div>\n
          What is the benefit of CHORUS for agencies?<\/b><\/div>\n
          \n
            \n
          • CHORUS minimizes compliance costs for agencies by integrating compliance with existing publishing practices and systems<\/li>\n
          • CHORUS provides a framework that minimizes development and operational costs for agencies, in keeping with OSTP’s requirement that resources must be identified within existing agency budgets<\/li>\n
          • CHORUS allows agencies to focus on their research funding mission rather than investing time and energy in building infrastructure that duplicates what already exists<\/li>\n
          • \u00a0OSTP recognized the integral contributions that scholarly publishers bring to scientific knowledge and new discoveries as well as the need for public-private collaboration.\u00a0 CHORUS provides an approach that preserves scholarly publishing through collaboration<\/li>\n
          • CHORUS can interface with any existing infrastructure or repository for identifying and linking to related content<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div>\n
            What is the benefit of CHORUS for universities, libraries and researchers?<\/b><\/div>\n
            \n
              \n
            • CHORUS provides access to research articles in a way that is familiar to the research community:\u00a0 finding them in the journals in which they are published.\u00a0 This streamlines the research effort, ensures that articles are viewable in context and allows for surveying the past topical literature<\/li>\n
            • CHORUS can interface with institutional repositories for identifying and linking to related content; CHORUS minimizes compliance costs for universities and researchers by integrating compliance with existing publishing practices and systems<\/li>\n
            • CHORUS protects limited research funding by eliminating the need for agencies to duplicate infrastructure<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/div>\n
              Additional Background<\/span><\/b><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              What is the cost of CHORUS?<\/b><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              The combined existing infrastructures of CrossRef and the CrossRef member publishers are capable of addressing the vast majority of the requirements articulated in the OSTP directive.\u00a0 Publishers have offered to subsume the remaining costs.\u00a0 Thus, the essential elements of the OSTP directive for public access to publications can be provided with little or no expenditure of federal agencies’ funds – preserving these resources for the direct funding of research.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              Additionally, the required CrossRef metadata to link to articles that report on federally-funded research are already available to the federal agencies, universities, research libraries and the public at no cost.\u00a0 FundRef is in place to allow the addition of metadata to identify these articles.\u00a0 More sophisticated interfaces could be developed according to agency needs with interfaces (APIs) and infrastructure provided by CHORUS.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              Governance and accountability<\/b><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              Publishers will be responsible for ensuring that each article subject to the public access policy becomes available to users on the appropriate date.\u00a0 Publishers will also provide listings of such articles to agencies and research institutions to satisfy their own accountability requirements.\u00a0 There will be back-up systems to ensure persistent access in case of compliance issues, whether technical or otherwise.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              In addition to publishers, CHORUS governance will include agency representatives to oversee high-level requirements, monitor implementation and maintain the essential interactions with agencies and connections to the US research community.\u00a0 CHORUS will also involve key stakeholder groups such as universities and research libraries to ensure these partners can easily and cost-effectively comply with the OSTP directive.\u00a0 Additionally, they will provide expertise in critical areas, such as digital preservation.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              Embargo periods<\/b><\/div>\n
              \u00a0<\/b><\/div>\n
              Articles will be available through CHORUS after the determined embargo for each discipline and agency.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              In its directive released in February 2013, OSTP suggested 12 months as a guideline for embargoes for posting peer-reviewed published journal articles but also cited the need for flexibility in setting the plan “(based on needs) unique to each field.”\u00a0\u00a0Publishers’ statement on OSTP and background<\/a><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              What is the status of CHORUS and what’s next?<\/b><\/b><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              The publishers that conceived CHORUS are in discussions with OSTP, the funding agencies, universities and research library communities.\u00a0 Agency and research university representatives have been invited to join the project teams.\u00a0 Our plan is to work out the system architecture and technical specifications over the summer and have an initial proof of concept completed by\u00a0August 30.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              About CrossRef and FundRef<\/span><\/b><\/div>\n
              \u00a0<\/span><\/b><\/div>\n
              What are FundRef and CrossRef and what are their roles in CHORUS?<\/b><\/div>\n
              \u00a0<\/b><\/div>\n
              CrossRef is a non-profit organization that oversees the international database of metadata on scholarly publications.\u00a0 It is responsible for enabling reference linking among the world’s scholarly publishing platforms.\u00a0 CrossRef has registered more than 60 million Digital Object Identifiers for electronic versions of research articles, book chapters and technical reports.<\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              CrossRef was launched in 2000 and now has more than 4300 participating scholarly publishing organizations from around the world, covering all scholarly disciplines.\u00a0 Participating publishers are collectively responsible for more than 27,000 journal titles that publish more than 1.8 million articles per year.\u00a0\u00a0Nearly 2000 libraries have signed up for free CrossRef accounts to take full advantage of CrossRef metadata; countless others (e.g. researchers) use CrossRef’s open interfaces, which don’t require signing up for an account, and services such as abstracting and indexing databases pay for access to support commercial services.\u00a0CrossRef website<\/a><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              CrossRef launched a production version of FundRef on May 27, 2013.\u00a0 This collaboration between publishers and three US funding agencies (DOE, NSF and NASA) identifies journal articles reporting research from federal funding through publisher-provided metadata.\u00a0 It will provide the essential back-end infrastructure for CHORUS.\u00a0\u00a0FundRef website<\/a><\/div>\n
              <\/div>\n
              CrossRef and, now, FundRef create the infrastructure and protocols that will establish a foundation for a streamlined, comprehensive public access distribution solution for federal agencies:\u00a0 one specifically designed to be nimble and scalable and to more efficiently serve the public demand for access without the burden of creating a new system from scratch.<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"

                Michael Eisen was born in Boston, but grew up in Bethesda, MD. He attended Walt Whitman High School<\/a> (class of 1985),\u00a0where he was captain of the math team and backup catcher on the baseball team. He was a member of the Montgomery County math team that won ARML<\/a> – the national high school math competition – in 1985. He went on the major in mathematics at Harvard College (class of 1989).<\/p>\n

                After college, he entered the graduate program in Biophysics at Harvard University, where he did his graduate work with Don C. Wiley<\/a> studying the function and evolution influenza virus proteins. He received his Ph.D. in 1996.<\/p>\n

                <\/a><\/p>\n

                Before continuing his scientific career, Dr. Eisen pursued a lifelong dream by becoming the play-by-play voice of the Columbia Mules<\/a>, a minor league baseball team in Columbia, TN.<\/p>\n

                <\/a><\/p>\n

                Following this sojourn, Dr. Eisen moved to Stanford where he joined the labs of Patrick Brown and David Bostein as a postdoctoral fellow and played a major role in the development and application of the DNA microarrays that were invented in the Brown lab. His 1998 paper<\/a> on the cluster analysis of gene expression data has been cited over 10,000 times (and is the 3rd most highly cited paper<\/a> in the 100 year history of the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences)<\/p>\n

                <\/a><\/p>\n

                In 2001 he moved to Berkeley, where he started his own lab – initially at the Lawrence Berkeley National Lab, and (as of 2006) the University of California, Berkeley<\/a>. His lab uses a combination of experimental, computational and evolutionary genomic techniques to study how genomes encode the complex spatial and temporal patterns of gene expression that drive animal development, using flies in the genus Drosophila<\/a><\/em> as a model system. He was named a Pew Scholar in the Biomedical Sciences <\/a>in 2001 (and now serves on their board), received a Presidential Early Carrer Award for Science and Engineering<\/a> in 2004 and was named an Investigator of the Howard Hughes Medical Institute<\/a> in 2008. He is currently an Associate Professor of Genetics, Genomics and Development in the Department of Molecular and Cell Biology<\/a>, and holds appointments in the Department of Integrative Biology<\/a> and Museum of Comparative Zoology<\/a>. The Eisen Lab website<\/a> describes ongoing research in the lab and has a lits of all of its publications.<\/p>\n

                Throughout his career, Dr. Eisen has worked to ensure that universal access to the products and benefits of scientific research. In 2000 he joined his former postdoctoral advisor Patrick Brown, and Nobel Prize winning cancer researcher Harold Varmus in launching the Public Library of Science<\/a> (PLoS), a San Francisco based non-profit advocacy organization and publisher dedicated to making the world’s scientific and medical literature a freely available public resource. PLoS aims to eliminate the subscription based model that dominates the $10b\/year scientific and medical publishing industry (and which denies access to the results of publicly funded research to students, teachers, physicians and countless others who would benefit from access to to latest scientific and medical discoveries) and to replace it with an “open access” model that makes all published papers immediately and universally freely available. To establish the viability of this model, PLoS has launched a series of open access journals, beginning with PLoS Biology<\/a> in 2003. Its journals have become highly successful (PLoS One<\/a>, launched in 2008, is now the largest scientific journal in the world, and will publish close to 10,000 articles in 2011) and PLoS has become a major force in reforming scientific publishing.<\/p>\n
