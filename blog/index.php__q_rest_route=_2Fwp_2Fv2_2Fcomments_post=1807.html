I agree with most of the points you make, and a lot of how my interest in “Open” knowledge has grown through reading your content. I think that when it comes to ownership of innovation the subject is convoluted by an idea that copyright is independently dynamic from its drafter. It is a legality that is as open as the person who drafts it allows it to be. In the U.S, anyone can register a Copyright. While you can download a template, or have a lawyer draft up a backbone, there is plenty of room for an innovator to decide how the article can be used and protected. The issues lie in the transfer of ownership by-proxy from the creator to a publisher that has the tools to get work recognized where an individual scientist may not. <\/p>\n

Scientific output needs to be available in order to advance knowledge. Scientists build on one another to solve problems that requires multiple viewpoints from experts. There is a reason why peer-review is in theory is the best way for scientists to come to a consensus on whether or not the work being presented can hold up to the authors resolve. We work hard as scientists, and academic science is harsh. There is very little freedom for those who contribute the knowledge, yet there is so much for others to gain. Enforcing a standard for quality assurance on those who define quality through innovation, is twisted. Scientists, especially those in academia, do not do it for capitalistic gain. The impact factor system is not necessary when it is in the product of scientific innovation which is beneficial for society. But somehow the impact factor system, a way to rate and put value on scientists and their work, is being driven by a mechanism that charges scientists an obscene amount of money to publish and obtain the copyright through publishing, all while selling it right back to the libraries of institutions where the science originated. In addition, they get to rank us on our societal worth. Which really comes to which scientists have the money to dish out high impact journals that will help them get a higher ranking. How would the editors in Nature, Cell or Science feel if the situation was reversed and they had to properly pay scientists for their innovations, and display an innovators copyright with clear acknowledgment in the subscriptions they sold, because that is how it should be if they are going to make millions off of science. <\/p>\n

Granted, I am not an expert in my field of science (bioengineering and metabolism), and I am still finding my own view in “Open-X.” I don’t think a copyright or CC license can be held responsible for the limitations of academic freedoms here, it just convolutes the reality that innovation is powerful for those who can exploit it. Innovation is better than money, because it has tangible value. Allowing innovation to be rated by those who do not innovate, but charge innovators to take the innovation off their hands so they can capitalize on subscriptions that only innovators subscribe to is beyond capitalism.<\/p>\n"},"link":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/?p=1807&cpage=1#comment-1502592","status":"approved","type":"comment","author_avatar_urls":{"24":"https:\/\/secure.gravatar.com\/avatar\/7d5ac4b4882988a233651dbecedd3f04?s=24&d=mm&r=g","48":"https:\/\/secure.gravatar.com\/avatar\/7d5ac4b4882988a233651dbecedd3f04?s=48&d=mm&r=g","96":"https:\/\/secure.gravatar.com\/avatar\/7d5ac4b4882988a233651dbecedd3f04?s=96&d=mm&r=g"},"meta":[],"_links":{"self":[{"href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=\/wp\/v2\/comments\/1502592"}],"collection":[{"href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=\/wp\/v2\/comments"}],"up":[{"embeddable":true,"post_type":"post","href":"https:\/\/www.michaeleisen.org\/blog\/index.php?rest_route=\/wp\/v2\/posts\/1807"}]}},{"id":1502497,"post":1807,"parent":0,"author":0,"author_name":"Bj\u00f6rn Brembs","author_url":"http:\/\/brembs.net","date":"2015-12-17T04:40:43","date_gmt":"2015-12-17T12:40:43","content":{"rendered":"
